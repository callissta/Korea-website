10Things you must know before going to South Korea


As everyone knows, Esepcially K-POP fans, South korea is often considered one of the most beautiful countries in Asia. It is a highly developed and modern country with many stunning places to visit, Such as Jeju Island, Gyeongju, Chunwang Peak, and Namsan Tower Not to mention the vibrant city of SEOUL at night.



1. Do not use Red Pen

In Korea the colour RED symbolises death, and red pen is used to record the names of people who have passed. This had led to a huge taboo around writing a living person's name in red pen it's seen as the equivalent of putting a curse on them or wishing for them to die.

So it would be a good idea to avoid red colour in South Korea.


2. Do not take photos of someone without permission


It's common when you see someone is pretty or handsome you might wanted to take a photo of them randomly, but unfortunately South Korea has strict privacy laws that protect individuals from unauthorized photography. So stop this behavior immediately when go to South Korea if you don't wanted to get report.

As a result, taking photos of strangers without permission can be seen as instrusive and disrespectful.


3. Don't stick chopsticks in rice


Leaving chopsticks stuck into rice is a faux pas in many East Asian cultures as they resemble the ceremonial incense sticks used at funerals. However, there are also practical considerations. Korean chopsticks are typically metal and heavier than bamboo or wooden chopsticks, So are likely to topple and flick rice all over your companions if you try to stick them in your rice bowl.

So it would be a good idea to avoid red colour in South Korea.


4. Don't wear revealing clothes


Although koreans are beginning to evolve and accept certain ways of dressing, there are still some clothing styles that are not really looked upon in South Korea. Exposed too much like barebacks or chest are considered slightly indecent.

However, it is totally fine to wear to show as much as legs as you want, you might noticed that korean women love wearing skirts, some of them pair the skirts with socks, leggings or simply wear them with boy shorts under the skirt.


5. Don't use one hand to receive things


Giving or receiving something with two hands is another gesture that korean people use to show respect to each other, and especially to their elders or superiors. You may experience this in stores too when a shopkeeper hands you your purchases, as they are grateful for your patronage.

If you're travelling for work, your korean colleagues may offer you a business card with both hands. In this case, the polite thing to do is mirror their body language and receive it with both hands.


6. Don't wear shoes indoors


Koreans place a high value an cleanliness, and removing shoes before entering a home helps maintain a clean and hygienic living space. The street can be dusty or dirty, and shoes can carry unwanted grime into the house.

By taking off your shoes, you show respect for the homeowner's space and contribute to a more comfortable enviroment for everyone.


7. Never speak loudly in public


In south korea, maintaining harmony and being considerate of others are highly valued. Speaking loudly in public places-such as on public transportation, in restaurant, or in crowded streets-is often seen as disruptive and impolite.

Koreans generally prefer a quieter atmosphere, and it's considered good manners to keep conversations at a moderate volume. This practice reflects the country's emphasis on respect for other's personal space and the collective need for a peaceful enviroment.


8. Avoid direct eye contact


In many Asian cultures, including south korea, direct eye contact can be perceived as a challenge or a sign of disrespect, particularly towards those who are older, it is customary to lower your gaze or make indirect eye contact as a sign of humility and deference.

Koreans consider it rude to look someone directly in the eye, cross your arms or legs, or have your hands in pocket when you are talking to them.


9. Don't litter or spit in public


The streets are kept remarkably clean in south korea, and citizens take great pride in their enviroment. Littering or spitting in public is not only disrespectful but can also result in fines. The government has implemented strict waste disposal practices, including separating trash into recyclables and specific categories.

Trash bins are sometimes scarce, so it's common for people to carry their waste until they can dispose of it properly.


10. Don't eat before the elderly


In south korea culture, elders hold a revered position, and showing respect to them in fundamental in social interactions. This respect during mealtime is by waiting for the oldest person at the table to begin eating before you take your first bite.

In korea, when you starting to eat before the elders can be perceived as rude or inconsiderate, as it may suggest a lack of regard for their authority and experience.
